Picturing the War

01 — Introduction

The primary arena of visual narrative contestation

Since Russia's full-scale invasion on 24 February 2022, Telegram has functioned as the central arena of visual narrative contestation in the conflict. Top pro-Kremlin channels accumulated 95.5 billion views in 2022 alone. Telegram citations across pro-Russian media surged by 481%. The platform did not merely transmit the war — it became a structural component of it.

Yet despite a growing body of scholarship on digital propaganda and platform-mediated witnessing, no study had systematically examined the visual content of this war at scale, across both sides, and over its full duration. This research addresses that gap.

Drawing on a corpus of approximately 24.000+ media items scraped from ten Telegram channels — five Russian and five Ukrainian — spanning February 2022 to February 2026, the study applies a structured annotation framework across seven analytical dimensions: content type, implied violence, gamification, dehumanization, aestheticization, narrative framing, and camera frame. By coding these dimensions symmetrically across channels ranging from state-affiliated institutions to independent milbloggers, it offers a comparative and longitudinal account of how wartime visual strategies evolved and diverged across four years of conflict.

"Despite sharp ideological opposition, Russian and Ukrainian channels share a common visual vocabulary — differing not in the types of imagery deployed, but in their narrative function."

The study is motivated by three questions: How do Russian and Ukrainian channels differ in their visual strategies, and have those differences converged or intensified over time? How has the technical mode of visual production shifted — particularly with the rise of drone warfare? And what structural relationships exist between the dimensions of visual propaganda?

02 — Literature Review

What prior scholarship establishes — and where it stops

The Russo-Ukrainian conflict is frequently characterised as a "hybrid war" in which digital platforms serve as primary theatres of narrative control, with Telegram emerging as its most consequential visual arena (Bareikytė & Skop, 2022). A foundational precedent was established by Makhortykh and Sydorova (2017), who demonstrated that image-based communication during the 2014 Donbas conflict intensified polarisation rather than producing shared understanding — a finding this study revisits at significantly greater scale and over a longer temporal frame.

The scale of Telegram's subsequent growth is documented by DFRLab (2024), whose analysis reveals a 481% increase in Telegram citations across pro-Kremlin media, with top channels accumulating 95.5 billion views in 2022 alone. RIA Novosti nearly tripled its annual viewership by 2023, while anonymous milblogger channels leveraged their opacity as a trust-building branding tactic. This infrastructure of mass visual dissemination was not improvised. Theisen et al. (2025) found an 8,925% surge in posts and a 5,352% increase in images shared by Russian milblogger accounts in the two weeks preceding the February 2022 invasion, suggesting that coordinated visual escalation on Telegram functions as a structural precursor to conflict rather than merely its by-product.

"Coordinated visual escalation on Telegram functions as a structural precursor to conflict rather than merely its by-product." — Theisen et al. (2025)

Asymmetric framing strategies

Research into the content of wartime visual production reveals deeply asymmetric framing on each side. Ptaszek et al. (2024) found that Russian institutional coverage maintained a stable technical-military register insulated from battlefield realities, while Ukrainian output remained more event-responsive — demonstrating how Telegram amplifies propagandistic framing rather than correcting for it. At the grassroots level, Bareikytė and Makhortykh (2024) show that the platform simultaneously enables authentic civilian testimony and strategic propaganda within overlapping channel ecosystems, a duality they term "inconspicuous digital witnessing."

Visually, content on both sides relies on metonymic strategies — ruins and soldiers substituting for abstract concepts of destruction — producing imagery that is "agonised, localized, and politicized" and designed to bridge psychological distance for international audiences (Nagy-Béni, 2025). Official and independent accounts alike deploy what Mejova et al. (2025) term "memetic warfare," mobilising archetypal hero/victim/villain narratives to shape opinion, with victim-centred imagery shown to generate significantly higher engagement.

Representation, disinformation, and epistemological instability

The broader visual economy of the conflict is constrained by a crisis of representation. Conventional coverage defaults to sensationalist clichés — tanks, explosions, devastation — that flatten the complexity of lived conflict (Sopova, 2021), while "war influencers" must navigate what Heřmanová et al. (2025) term "ambivalent visibility," reconciling the ethics of witnessing with a platform logic that rewards spectacular subject matter.

This tension is compounded by a disinformation environment of unprecedented scale. DFRLab (2024) documents how anonymous channels use their opacity to circulate manipulated imagery structurally difficult to attribute or refute, while Standaert (2024) shows that Telegram functions as an "anteroom" where fabricated content consolidates before migrating to mainstream platforms. The deployment of deepfakes — including those targeting President Zelenskyy — further demands computational detection methods to distinguish authentic from synthetic footage (Boháček & Farid, 2022), while the platform's archival instability makes civic archiving essential for preserving wartime visual evidence (Bareikytė & Skop, 2022).

The methodological gap this study addresses

The existing scholarship, while establishing critical foundations, leaves a significant methodological gap. Studies are predominantly qualitative, focus on a single actor or platform, or rely on text-mining that treats visual content as secondary. No prior study has systematically annotated a large-scale, longitudinal corpus of visual media drawn simultaneously from Russian and Ukrainian Telegram channels across the full duration of the 2022–2026 conflict.

This research addresses that gap by scraping and annotating approximately 24.000+ media items from ten channels — five Russian, five Ukrainian — spanning state-affiliated institutions to independent milbloggers, and coding for violence, aesthetic register, and content type over time. By applying this framework symmetrically to both sides, the study offers a comparative, longitudinal account of how visual strategies have evolved and mutually responded across the war's duration — directly engaging questions raised but unanswered by the DFRLab (2024), whose aggregate platform patterns are here grounded in granular visual content analysis.

04 — Methodology

Five analytical pipelines, one shared framework

The study's analytical architecture rests on a single shared foundation: a corpus of approximately 24.000+ annotated media items scored across seven dimensions. From this corpus, five complementary lenses are applied, each designed to address a distinct research sub-question. The following sections describe the data collection and annotation process, then present each analytical component in the order it was executed.

I. Data Collection

The corpus was assembled using a custom Python script built on the Telethon library, which interfaces with the Telegram API to retrieve message metadata programmatically. The ten channels were selected to represent a range of pro-Russian and pro-Ukrainian information ecosystems, including front-line reporting accounts, official military-linked channels, and occupied-territory outlets. The scraping pipeline was designed to collect media-bearing posts — photographs, videos (retrieved via thumbnail), and documents — published between 24 February 2022 and 24 February 2026. For each message, the following metadata was extracted: channel identifier, publication date, media type, file size, view count, forward count, reply count, and user reactions.

To ensure temporal representativeness while managing data volume, a systematic sampling method was applied: up to 50 evenly spaced media posts per channel per month were selected, yielding a corpus of approximately 24.000+ sampled observations. All cross-channel and cross-temporal comparisons are therefore proportional rather than absolute — a deliberate methodological constraint that precludes analysis of absolute publication volume but enables reliable comparative analysis of content composition over time.

II. Content Annotation Pipeline

A structured annotation framework was developed to systematically code each media item across seven analytical dimensions, enabling standardised comparative analysis across channels and time. The codebook captures both descriptive and interpretive features of each image or video.

The Seven Annotation Dimensions

Each image received a score on all seven dimensions simultaneously via a single API call returning a structured JSON object.

Content type — primary subject (combatants, equipment, territory, casualties, etc.)
Implied violence — degree to which content suggests or depicts harm (0–10)
Gamification — visual/rhetorical elements framing warfare in ludic or competitive terms (0–10)
Dehumanization — representations stripping enemy actors of human attributes (0–10)
Aestheticization — degree to which violence or destruction is rendered visually appealing (0–10)
Narrative framing — broader discursive register (victorious, defensive, martyrological, etc.) (0–10)
Camera frame / POV — compositional and perspectival choices shaping viewer identification

The pipeline was developed in three stages. A first batch was annotated using the Claude API as a gold-standard reference. The research team then manually coded 50 images, which were fed into the model as few-shot demonstrations to transfer codebook logic to the automated pipeline. Final annotation at scale was carried out using GPT. A recurring challenge was the model's reluctance to code violent content due to built-in safety constraints; this was addressed by prompting the model to adopt the persona of an academic researcher in conflict studies, which proved effective in eliciting consistent annotations across the full dataset.

III. 2D Visual Map

To provide an exploratory overview of the corpus structure prior to annotation, a 2D visual map was produced by passing 400 manually selected images through CLIP (ViT-B-32), a vision-language model that generates high-dimensional semantic embeddings for visual content. The 400 images were selected to ensure representativity both across channels and across content type. Embeddings were computed using the sentence-transformers library, and dimensionality reduction was applied via t-SNE (scikit-learn) to produce a two-dimensional scatter plot. The pipeline was implemented in Python, executed in Google Colab. The resulting map was used to inform the categories of the annotation codebook and to identify the primary visual clusters in the corpus before large-scale annotation.

IV. Channel Content Analysis

The primary goal of the channel content analysis is to identify shifts in communication strategies across individual Telegram channels over the four-year observation period. By normalising the data (posts by channel and month) into percentages, the relative importance of different content types — combat, civilian victims, propaganda, political commentary, drone strikes — can be compared across channels and over time without the confounding effects of differential posting frequency.

Results are rendered as interactive Plotly heatmaps with a shared colour scale ranging from 0% to 80%. The scale is capped at 80% to accommodate the extreme dominance of certain categories in certain channels (notably the political category in wargonzo, which reaches above 60% in some months) while preserving gradient resolution for the more commonly observed 0–40% range. Each cell in the heatmaps represents the percentage share of a given content type within a given channel in a given month. The interface offers two complementary views: a "category" view presenting one heatmap per content type (allowing cross-channel comparison) and a "channel" view presenting one heatmap per channel (allowing holistic reading of each channel's editorial profile over time).

V. Violence Analysis

Of the seven annotated dimensions, implied violence was selected for dedicated in-depth analysis. Violence is the dimension most directly constitutive of wartime visual communication — present across content types, channels, and time periods in ways that cut across all other analytical categories. Its dedicated treatment is further justified by the density of interpretive information available: beyond the numerical score, each annotation includes a justification text describing the specific visual cues that informed the rating, enabling qualitative analysis at scale.

The analysis is structured in two stages. The first is quantitative, examining four aspects of the violence scores across the corpus: their overall distribution, their evolution over the four-year observation period, their variation across channels and between Russian and Ukrainian channel groups, and their relationship to post engagement as measured by view counts. The second stage is qualitative, constructing an inductive typology of violence by systematically reading annotation descriptions and grouping images by shared visual logic — rather than relying solely on the model's content type classifications. This process identified eight distinct visual genres through which violence is represented across the corpus, ranging from direct casualty imagery to aestheticised memorial memes (the Груз 200 genre). All results are presented through an interactive web-based visualisation developed specifically for this analysis, allowing dynamic exploration across time, channel, and violence category.

VI. Technical Means Analysis

To track shifts in the technical mode of visual representation over time, images were grouped into half-year periods (H1/H2) from the first half of 2022 through the second half of 2025, yielding eight time bins. The partial 2026 period (989 images) was excluded to avoid truncation bias. The primary dependent variable is frame type, a seven-category classification of how each image was technically produced: drone POV, soldier POV, civilian recording, professional production, composite/meme, screenshot, and other.

For each period, proportional shares were calculated at both the aggregate corpus level and per individual channel, to distinguish corpus-wide trends from channel-specific behaviour — a two-level approach necessary because aggregate shifts can be driven by a small number of high-volume channels rather than reflecting uniform change. As a secondary lens, mean scores for the five continuous annotation dimensions were computed per period and cross-tabulated by frame type, to assess whether changing frame composition corresponds to qualitative shifts in how violence is represented.

VII. Correlatory Effects

To explore correlatory effects across the five annotation dimensions, a series of bivariate ordinary least squares (OLS) regression analyses were conducted on the full annotated corpus of 24.000+ images. For each model, one score was designated the independent variable and a theoretically related score the dependent variable, drawing on prior literature on visual propaganda and conflict media to motivate each pairing. Pearson correlation coefficients, regression slopes, intercepts, and R² values were computed, and statistical significance was assessed via p-values derived from the t-distribution on the regression slope. Three targeted models were constructed: (1) gamification → implied violence; (2) dehumanization → implied violence; (3) aestheticization → narrative framing. To contextualise each relationship, mean scores per discrete score level were computed with 95% confidence intervals (±1.96 × σ/√n), and results were disaggregated by content type, frame type, and channel. A full 5×5 Pearson correlation heatmap was produced to identify second-order relationships across the score network.

05 — Finding I

A shared iconographic language

The 2D map reveals two overlapping axes of clustering. The first is content-based: images group into broad visual categories regardless of source channel, including geographic maps and infographic cards, military portraiture, hardware and weapons systems, and scenes of physical destruction. The second axis, partly overlaid on the first, reflects channel affiliation.

Both sides draw on the same visual
pool of maps, hardware,
portraiture, and destruction

Russian channels cluster heavily around maps and operational infographics, heroic soldier portraiture, and technological warfare imagery. This visual repertoire displays the war as strategic and controlled, highlighting territorial gains and framing the soldier as a heroic agent, in line with Soviet propaganda conventions. Ukrainian channels, by contrast, cluster around portraits of dead Russian soldiers and scenes of physical destruction photographed in a documentary register, oriented toward witness and accountability rather than narrative triumph.

The map therefore does not sort cleanly along a single dimension. Content type remains the clearest organizing force, but it is inflected by a second pattern of channel affiliation that distinguishes strategic-display imagery from documentary aftermath.

06 — Finding II

Russian channels talk politics. Ukrainian channels document destruction.

The most structurally significant finding from the channel content analysis is the divergence between Russian and Ukrainian channels in their balance of political versus kinetic content (political here referring to ideological commentary, state messaging, and interpretive framing rather than battlefield events themselves).

Initial observations suggest that in the early stages of the conflict, content types were highly fragmented. Over time, channels began to specialise their messaging, visible in the channel-view heatmaps as increasingly stable horizontal bands. The heatmaps therefore show not only group-level divergence between Russian and Ukrainian channels, but also the emergence of distinct channel-specific posting cultures.

The Russian pattern

Russian channels — particularly wargonzo and RVvoenkor — show a sustained and growing orientation toward political commentary. Wargonzo reached 40–67% political content share for most of the observation period. The pattern holds at lower magnitude across all Russian channels, suggesting it is a feature of the Russian military blogger ecosystem as a whole rather than individual editorial choices.

In early 2022, content was highly fragmented. Over time, Russian channels progressively consolidated around ideology, commentary, and framing — the war as argument rather than event.

The Ukrainian pattern

Ukrainian channels show more variation. A_shtirlitz is the notable exception, combining high political shares with exceptional propaganda meme volumes in 2022 — a combination consistent with an explicit information-warfare function. Channels anchored in occupied territories — donbassrealii and kherson_non_fake — are dominated by destruction and civilian content, reflecting their geographic position under sustained bombardment. Their editorial profile is not a choice but a condition.

The civilian category remains consistently more prominent across Ukrainian channels than Russian ones. Humanising the civilian population is a structural narrative priority that Russian channels largely subordinate to military, political, and ideological content.

07 — Finding III

Violence is a Ukrainian documentary reality, not a Russian narrative choice

The violence analysis produces the study's most counterintuitive result. Across the full corpus, the mean implied violence score is 4.23 out of 10, with a strikingly bimodal distribution — clustering around scores 1–4, dropping at 5, then spiking again at 6 and 8. The valley at 5 likely reflects a threshold effect in the annotation model, treating moderate and explicit violence as categorically distinct. Most consequentially, 41% of all images score 6 or above — the threshold at which violence shifts from implied to explicit — meaning that nearly one in two posts depicts violence in unambiguous terms.

4.31 mean violence score,
Ukrainian channels

Ukrainian channels score higher on implied violence than Russian channels (mean 4.16) — inverting the common assumption that violent imagery is primarily a feature of Russian information aggression. The two channels with the highest violence scores in the entire corpus are both Ukrainian: donbassrealii (4.73) and kherson_non_fake (4.59). Both are anchored in Russian-occupied territories. They document violence inflicted upon their communities — producing a visual register of destruction and aftermath that systematically outscores the operational and propagandistic content dominant on Russian channels.

Violence, in other words, is not primarily a Russian narrative choice in this corpus — it is a Ukrainian documentary reality. This distinction is consequential for how international audiences interpret the visual output of the conflict.

The normalisation curve

Mean violence scores rise from approximately 3.8 in February 2022 to stabilise around 4.3–4.6 from mid-2022 onwards, with a notable peak in early 2023 coinciding with the Battle of Bakhmut. Rather than escalating progressively, the visual register of violence reaches a plateau relatively early and then persists. Normalisation is rapid and durable, not gradual.

"Extreme violence does not drive engagement in the way platform logic predicts. Mean view counts peak at score 4 and decline at higher scores — violent content circulates in narrower, self-selecting networks rather than reaching mass audiences."

The qualitative typology further identifies eight distinct visual genres of violence, ranging from drone strike footage — characterised by a gamified HUD aesthetic that abstracts killing into a targeting interface — to Груз 200 memorial memes, in which portrait photographs of soldiers are overlaid with flame effects and death notation, producing aestheticised grief without graphic imagery.

Drone imagery rose from 3.4% of all images in early 2022 to a peak of 12% in H1 2024, before declining to 6.1% by late 2025. This shift was not uniform — it was driven by battlefield-proximate channels like ZA_FROHT, voenacher, and rusich_army, which reached 29–31% drone content at peak. Commentary channels never exceeded 5%. The drone shift is a property of combat-proximate channels, not the ecosystem as a whole.

The rise came at the direct expense of civilian recording, which fell from 41% to 28%. The war's dominant visual perspective shifted from the horizontal, ground-level viewpoint of a bystander to the vertical, overhead viewpoint of a drone operator. The camera moved from witnessing violence to being embedded in the system producing it. Soldier POV, despite GoPro proliferation, declined from 3.5% to 2% — the drone displaced rather than complemented the helmet cam.

Drone footage is not just more common — it is qualitatively different. HUD overlays, targeting reticles, and top-down framing reduce human figures to points of impact, producing a visual register closer to a video game than to documentation. Dehumanization scores within drone content hit 5.0 against a corpus average of 2.1. These are not marginal gaps. The technical mode of image production is not neutral — the same event filmed from the ground and from a drone generates fundamentally different representations of violence.

The partial retreat from the 2024 peak likely reflects both electronic-warfare countermeasures reducing successful strike recordings and a possible novelty decay, as composite and meme content rose simultaneously. The visual economy of the war therefore does not follow a linear trajectory toward technological mediation; it exhibits a rise-plateau-retreat pattern shaped by operational constraints and audience dynamics. Aggregate gamification and aestheticization scores also trended upward across frame types, suggesting a broader normalisation of stylised violence representation beyond any single technology.

09 — Finding V

Dehumanization is the load-bearing variable

Three targeted OLS regression models were run on the full corpus of 24.000+ images. For each model, one annotation score was designated the independent variable, with a theoretically motivated dependent variable. The results reveal a layered structure in which dehumanization functions as the central organising variable across the entire annotation network.

The weakest model — gamification onto implied violence (r=0.327, R²=0.107) — is almost entirely driven by drone strike footage, which records the highest mean gamification score of any content type at 6.88. Other content types scoring highly on implied violence, including casualty imagery and explosion footage, score near zero on gamification. The gamified register is a specialised visual grammar confined to a specific technological framing of warfare, not a pervasive feature of violent imagery.

The dehumanization model is substantially stronger (r=0.567, R²=0.321) — approximately three times the explanatory power of gamification. The mean implied violence score rises steadily from 2.28 at dehumanization score zero to 9.0 at dehumanization score ten, with no plateau effect at any intermediate level. Casualty imagery is the key driver, recording a mean dehumanization score of 4.59 and a mean implied violence score of 7.78 — the highest of any content type.

"Across the full correlation heatmap, dehumanization correlates with every other dimension at r = 0.43 to 0.59 — functioning as a mediating pathway through which other visual registers partly produce their effects."

The aestheticization-to-narrative framing model is the strongest (r=0.581, R²=0.338). Visual polish and ideological messaging are tightly coupled in this content ecosystem. Propaganda memes are the decisive outlier: mean scores of 6.64 on aestheticization and 7.27 on narrative framing — values entirely detached from every other content type. Notably, screenshots including tactical maps record a narrative framing mean of 4.29 despite a modest aestheticization score of 3.40, indicating that ideological messaging can travel through information design without requiring high visual production values.

10 — Discussion

What the findings resist

The visual dimension of this conflict operates according to logics that are neither straightforwardly propagandistic nor uniformly escalatory. The analytical frameworks inherited from prior work require revision when applied at scale.

On the shared visual grammar

The cluster map finding challenges the intuition that wartime visual production is fundamentally differentiated along national lines. Both functions — propaganda and witnessing — draw on a common pool of visual tropes, suggesting the conflict has generated a shared iconographic language that both sides inhabit differently rather than separately.

On violence as documentary condition

The finding that Ukrainian channels score higher on implied violence inverts the common framing of the conflict's information war. The data reveals a structural asymmetry: Russian channels subordinate violence to operational and ideological framing; Ukrainian channels in Kherson and Donbas document destruction as an ongoing reality. Violence here is not principally a rhetorical choice but an empirical condition of certain communities. The result also suggests that asymmetry in violent visual output reflects asymmetry in material experience rather than simply propagandistic intent.

On the drone camera

The drone analysis offers the clearest evidence of technology reshaping representation. The drone camera does not merely document violence — it reframes it within a targeting interface that strips human figures of individuality and consequence. This may be the most transformative representational shift in this conflict: not deepfake fabrication, but the routinisation of a real visual grammar that produces analogous epistemological effects through legitimate footage.

On dehumanization as policy lever

If dehumanization functions as a structural carrier for the other dimensions of propaganda — violence, aestheticization, narrative framing — then targeting it specifically, rather than addressing individual content types in isolation, may offer greater analytical and policy leverage for counter-disinformation interventions. The r=0.43 to 0.59 correlation range across all five dimensions is a finding with direct implications for media literacy design.

On engagement and virality

The finding that engagement peaks at moderate violence scores and declines at extreme levels also complicates familiar platform-logic assumptions about virality. Extreme violence appears to circulate within narrower, self-selecting networks rather than driving maximum reach, which has implications for how researchers and policymakers model the spread of harmful wartime content online.

11 — Limitations

What this study cannot claim

Several limitations must be acknowledged. The study is best understood as an exploratory contribution rather than a definitive empirical account. Its primary value lies in demonstrating the methodological feasibility of large-scale automated visual analysis in conflict contexts, and in generating hypotheses for more rigorously resourced future research.

Sampling and volume

The sampling method — capped at 50 posts per channel per month — ensures temporal comparability but precludes any analysis of absolute engagement or publication volume. All cross-channel and cross-temporal comparisons are therefore proportional rather than absolute. Channels that post more frequently are not systematically better represented, which is methodologically defensible but means the study cannot speak to questions of reach or total output.

Annotation quality

The automated annotation pipeline rests on a training set of only 50 manually coded images, which introduces subjectivity into the scoring framework. The codebook dimensions — particularly implied violence, aestheticization, and dehumanization — involve inherently interpretive judgements that are relative to this dataset and difficult to replicate without access to the original training material. A technical limitation was also observed whereby the model displayed a tendency to avoid extreme scores, particularly at the upper end of scales, which may compress variance in the results. Future iterations should incorporate larger and more systematically validated training sets, drawing on established academic literature on automated image analysis in conflict contexts; improved annotation quality would also benefit from dedicated funding.

Corpus scope

The corpus is limited to ten Telegram channels — a fraction of the Russian and Ukrainian military information ecosystems, which each comprise dense networks of channels that actively repost and amplify each other's content. Channels that are less visible, operate under pseudonyms, or have since been deleted are systematically excluded, meaning the dataset skews toward the most prominent and stable accounts. Moreover, restricting the analysis to Telegram leaves aside VKontakte, Twitter/X, and YouTube, which play significant roles in wartime visual communication.

Video treatment

Video content constitutes a significant share of posts on military Telegram channels. For operational and computational reasons, the pipeline processes only the thumbnail image extracted from each video rather than the full audiovisual file. This introduces a substantial reduction in analytical depth: a single static frame cannot capture narrative progression, motion effects, editing rhythm, or the affective dimensions of sound — music, explosions, voiceovers, silence — all of which are central to how war videos construct meaning and emotional impact. The drone POV findings in particular may be systematically underestimated, as the gamified qualities of drone footage are often most pronounced in the dynamic visual elements of the full clip.

12 — Conclusion

Threads made visible

This study set out to examine how visual content — violence, aesthetic register, content type — is represented across a longitudinal corpus of 24.000+ media items from ten Telegram channels spanning four years of conflict. Several findings stand out.

The channel content analysis shows that Russian channels have progressively oriented toward political commentary, while Ukrainian channels anchored in occupied territories maintain a documentary register dominated by destruction and civilian harm. The violence analysis confirms that explicit violent imagery is pervasive — in 41% of all posts — but its distribution is uneven in analytically significant ways.

The study is best understood as generative rather than conclusive. Its primary contribution is to have made these threads visible — and to have demonstrated the methodological feasibility of large-scale automated visual analysis in conflict contexts. Each analytical thread opened here merits dedicated follow-up research: the gamification of drone warfare, the Груз 200 memorial genre, the relationship between violence and engagement, the editorial divergence between Russian and Ukrainian channels.

Several directions for further research follow directly from these findings and limitations. Future work should expand the corpus substantially, both in channel volume to capture the broader and more volatile ecosystem of smaller milblogger accounts, and in platform diversity by extending the analysis to VKontakte, Twitter/X, and YouTube where complementary visual strategies operate. The annotation framework would also benefit from grounding in established theoretical literature on visual framing, propaganda aesthetics, and conflict photography, moving beyond inductive category construction toward hypotheses derived from prior research.

The treatment of video content remains an unresolved limitation: analysing full audiovisual material rather than static thumbnails would substantially deepen the analysis of how sound, editing rhythm, and motion construct meaning in wartime posts. More broadly, the study maps the terrain sufficiently to identify where deeper investigation is warranted; what it offers is not closure, but a clearer set of problems for subsequent research to pursue.

"The most consequential shift may not be what is being shown, but from where — the camera has moved from witnessing violence to being embedded in the system producing it."
